## Overview

SARS-CoV-2 transmission rates varied dramatically across U.S. states and over time during the COVID-19 pandemic. Understanding the structure of that variation — and what drove it — is essential for improving epidemic forecasting and informing future public health responses.

Using **Bayesian hierarchical models** and surveillance case data from 10 U.S. states, we are estimating how transmission rates changed over the course of the pandemic and how that variation was distributed across geography and time. Hierarchical models are well suited to this problem because they allow us to pool information across states while still capturing state-level differences — improving estimates in places with sparse data while accounting for the full range of variation across the country.

## Methods

Bayesian statistical modeling allows us to rigorously quantify uncertainty in our estimates. Rather than producing a single best-guess transmission rate, the models generate a probability distribution over plausible values — making it possible to see not just *what* transmission rates were, but how confident we can be in those estimates. This is especially important for public health decisions that depend on understanding the range of plausible scenarios.
