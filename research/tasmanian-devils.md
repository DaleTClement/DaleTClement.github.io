#### Will transmissible cancer drive Tasmanian devils to extinction?

For over 25 years, Tasmanian devils have experienced severe declines due to a unique malady: the devil facial tumor disease (DFTD), a fatal cancer that evolved the ability to jump from one devil to another and has since spread across the entire island of Tasmania. 

Early during the epidemic, when the declines in devil populations were most pronounced, there was widespread concern that DFTD would drive devils to extinction. Despite these initial predictions, Tasmanian devils have managed to survive, though in greatly reduced numbers. 

The future of devil conservation hinges on this critical question: *What has allowed Tasmanian devils to survive thus far and will it hold into the future?*

#### Using mechanistic models to predict the fate of Tasmanian devils 


My research focused on one of the leading hypotheses: *That over the course of a few generations, Tasmanian devils had evolved resistance to the transmission and growth of the tumor disease.* Genetic studies had shown that both devils and tumors were undergoing rapid evolution, but the overall effect of this evolution on the long-term prospects for species remained unclear. 

To answer this question, I worked with a team of experts in Tasmanian devil biology to build and validate a mathematical model that predicted the risk that DFTD would drive Tasmanian devils to extinction. Crucially, this was the first model to account for the effect of coevolution between devils and tumors on devil extinction risk. 

**We found that evolution could, indeed, play a major role in promoting long-term survival of the Tasmanian devil** and that it is critical for policy makers to account for these dynamics when setting devil conservation priorities. We also identified major areas of uncertainty that inhibited our ability to make more concrete predictions, setting priorities for future research on this iconic species.

#### Broader implications of our findings

Emerging infectious diseases are a major threat to many wildlife species and our results add to a growing body of evidence that rapid evolution can play a critical role in determining whether these species persist or go extinct. Beyond conservation science, interactions between rapid evolution and population dynamics appear everywhere from viral evolution (as with SARS-CoV-2) to the spread of antibiotic resistance to the adaptation of weed species to herbicide. "Eco-evolutionary" modeling is therefore an essential tool for disciplines ranging from public heath to agriculture.
