### When does trying not to get sick increase disease spread?

When people hear that an outbreak is spreading in their area, they often change how they move around: staying home more, avoiding crowded or high-risk places (like bars and restaurants), or spending more time in areas they perceive as safer (like outdoors). Public health messaging typically assumes these responses reduce disease spread. But does cautious behavior always help?

The challenge is that people cannot directly measure their risk of infection as they go about their daily lives. They rely on imperfect *proxies* for infection risk, like the number of disease cases reported in their municipality, how crowded a place looks, or features of the environment like ventilation. If the information people rely on to make decisions is not reflective of true infection risk, people may behave in ways that they think will reduce their chances of getting sick but in fact increase disease spread.


### What information might people use assess disease risk and how might they respond?


We built mathematical models to study how individual movement decisions interact with epidemic dynamics. For our primary use case, we focused on people who make day trips from home to other local destinations, and who adjust their travel habits based on perceived disease risk. Crucially, we examined how access to reliable, but incomplete, information affected the efficacy of changing travel behavior to avoid disease risk.

We found that people are most likely to behave in ways that fuel the spread of disease when they ignore important components of disease transmission (like ventilation in indoor locations) or focus on information irrelevant to actual infection risk. This is particularly relevant for emergent diseases, in which the exact mechanism of transmission is unknown, or in cases were there is substantial misinformation.

### What does this mean for predicting disease transmission?

Many epidemic models fail to incorporate behavioral responses to disease risk and those that do often assume that people base their behavior on reliable measure of risk, like reported cases. However, we know that this is often not the case, especially when individuals are making small-scale travel decisions, like which grocery store or restaurant to go to. Our findings suggest the effectiveness of risk-averse behavior depends on the specific disease context and the quality of information people have access to. Public health messaging should be designed with these nuances in mind — and forecasting tools should account for imperfect information when modeling how populations respond to outbreaks.
