## The Problem

When people hear that an outbreak is spreading in their area, they often change how they move around — staying home more, avoiding certain places, or traveling to locations they perceive as safer. Public health messaging typically assumes these responses reduce disease spread. But does cautious behavior always help?

The challenge is that people cannot directly measure their risk of infection. They rely on **proxies** — imperfect signals like how many sick people they've heard about in their neighborhood, how crowded a place looks, or features of the environment. Whether those proxies accurately reflect true transmission risk depends on the specific disease and setting.

## What We Did

We built mathematical models to study how individual movement decisions interact with epidemic dynamics. We focused on a realistic scenario: people who make day trips from a home location to other destinations, and who adjust their travel habits based on perceived disease risk.

We tested three types of risk proxies:
- **Local prevalence** — how many people nearby are visibly sick
- **Local host density** — how crowded a location is
- **Place-based risk** — inherent features of a location that affect transmission

We also considered two types of behavioral responses:
- **Absolute response** — simply staying home more often
- **Relative response** — shifting travel to locations that seem safer

## What We Found

- **Staying home more** (absolute response) was consistently effective at reducing disease spread
- **Shifting travel to "safer" locations** (relative response) sometimes *increased* transmission
- Harmful outcomes occurred when the risk proxy people used was **mismatched** with how the disease actually spreads — for example, avoiding crowded places when crowding is not actually what drives transmission
- The mode of disease transmission (whether it depends on crowd size or on the fraction of people who are sick) was a critical factor in determining whether behavioral responses helped or hurt

## Why It Matters

Epidemic models that assume behavioral responses are always beneficial will produce inaccurate forecasts. Our findings suggest that the effectiveness of risk-averse behavior depends on the specific disease context and the quality of information people have access to. Public health messaging should be designed with these nuances in mind — and forecasting tools should account for imperfect information when modeling how populations respond to outbreaks.
