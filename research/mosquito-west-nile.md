## Overview

West Nile Virus (WNV) is the most common mosquito-borne disease in the continental United States. It is transmitted to humans by *Culex* mosquitoes, and its spread depends on a complex mix of environmental factors: temperature, rainfall, land use, and the local abundance of bird species that serve as hosts. Because risk varies so much across space and time, effective public health response requires detailed, local knowledge of *where* and *when* mosquito populations and WNV activity are highest.

## The Project

In collaboration with the **Forsyth County Department of Public Health**, we are building spatial and temporal models of mosquito abundance and West Nile Virus activity across the county. The project integrates mosquito trap data, environmental covariates, and WNV surveillance data to identify patterns of elevated risk and their environmental drivers.

The results will help public health officials target mosquito surveillance and control interventions more efficiently — concentrating resources in the places and times where they will have the greatest impact.
