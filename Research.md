#### Mosquito and West Nile Virus Distribution in Forsyth County

In collaboration with the Forsyth County Department of Public Health, we are mapping when and where mosquito populations and West Nile Virus activity are highest in the county to support targeted public health interventions. This project is ongoing, so I'll have a lot more to say about it in the future.

#### [How Movement Decisions Shape Disease Spread](/research/behavioral-epidemiology/)

<img src="/assets/images/Methods Snippet - Website.svg" alt="Schematic of 'Simple Trips' host movement" style="float: right; margin: 0 0 1.5em 1.5em; width: 200px; border-radius: 4px;">

As the COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated, people change how often they travel and where they go during disease outbreaks — but their perception of risk doesn't always match how the disease actually spreads. We show that risk-avoidant changes in travel can sometimes *increase* transmission if there is a mismatch between perceived and actual risk.

#### [The Evolution and Persistence of Tasmanian Devils and their Transmissible Cancer](\research/tasmanian-devils/)

<figure style="float: right; margin: 0 0 1.5em 1.5em; width: 200px;"> <img src="/assets/images/Sarcophilus_harrisii_taranna.jpg" alt="Photo of a Tasmanian Devil" style="display: block; width: 100%; border-radius: 4px;">
<figcaption> Photo credit: JJ Harison. Accessed from <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sarcophilus_harrisii_taranna.jpg">Wikipedia</a>. CC BY-SA 3.0 </figcaption> </figure>

Tasmanian devils are at risk of extinction from a fatal, transmissible cancer. At the same time, there is growing genomic evidence that devil are rapidly evolving resistance to the cancer. Using mechanistic modeling and two decades' worth of data, we show that this rapid evolution could play a major role in continued survival of Tasmanian devils.

**[Learn More →](/research/tasmanian-devils/)**, or read our **[full paper](<https://doi.org/10.1093/evolut/qpae143>)** published in *Evolution*.

#### Adaptation of natural populations to rapid environmental change

My graduate research examined the role of environmental variability in determining whether a natural population can successfully respond to environmental change. To cope with environmental variability, organisms must be able to predict future conditions using environmental cues and adjust their behavior or physiology accordingly. Human-altered environments often change the timing and frequency of environmental cues, which may lead organisms to respond inappropriately to the new conditions. My dissertation work found that phenotypic plasticity in traits such as anti-predator behavior or germination time is most likely to become maladaptive when environmental change causes previously accurate cues, which the population had evolved to rely upon, suddenly become unreliable. See my [Publications](/Publications/) for details.
